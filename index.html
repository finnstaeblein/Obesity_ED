The Obesity Myths:

We have to be more clear about the
Cause of Obesity

Global obesity has risen dramtically over the past several decades. Despite substantial knowledge about the mechanism behind obesity, the reasons behind this rise are still heavily debated. Scroll to explore why what Americans believe about obesity might not match the data.

Obesity has increased worldwide

Obesity has increased almost everywhere over the past few decades. Estimates from the World Health Organization indicate that global adult obesity has nearly tripled since 1975, reaching about 1 in 8 adults worldwide by 2022. [4]

While the timing and speed of rising obesity differ across countries, obesity is becoming more prevelant globally. This raises two questions: 1) why is it increasing, and 2) what explains the differences between countries?

1/2

What is causing this rise?

At its core, obesity occurs when energy intake is consistently higher than energy expenditure. The harder question is which side of that balance has shifted in the last few decades. Public health organizations typically point to two possibilities: people may be burning less energy through daily activity, or they may be consuming more energy as diets shift. [1]

What do most you think is the most important reason many Americans are very overweight?

2/2

Lower activity?

A 2006 Pew survey captured Americans’ beliefs about what was causing obesity. Most respondents pointed to personal behavior as the main driver, and they overwhelmingly pointed to one explanation in particular: not getting enough exercise. Specifically, they rated lack of activity as the most important reason people are overweight, ahead of diet, marketing, or genetics. [2] Although the study is almost a decade old, it captures a then dominant view among Americans, shared with health organizations, that declining physical activity is the major contributor to rising obesity. [1]

Examining the Activity Hypothesis

A cross-cultural study published in PNAS directly tested the assumption that rising obesity is driven by declines in physical activity. The researchers measured total daily energy expenditure using the doubly labeled water method, which captures how much energy people actually burn in their everyday lives. The data were drawn from the International Atomic Energy Agency’s global database and supplemented with three additional populations: the Aymara in Bolivia, the Tuvan in Siberia, and the Daasanach in northern Kenya. [1]

TThe final study sample included 4,213 adults across 34 population groups. While the original researchers had access to individual-level measurements, access to those data requires a formal application. This analysis therefore relies on the published population means. Importantly, each population represents a community-level sample rather than a national estimate.

We included hunter gatherers, pastoralists, horticulturalists, small scale farmers, and industrialized groups because they differ in lifestyle, and market integration. These differences allow meaningful comparisons across variables that may predict obesity levels. To measure obesity, we used body fat percentage rather than body mass index to avoid misclassifying individuals with higher muscle mass.

Body Fat vs Physical Activity Level

This plot compares body fat percentage with Physical Activity Level (PAL), which is total energy expenditure divided by basal energy expenditure. PAL gives a size-adjusted measure of daily activity.

For men, the slope is strongly negative and statistically significant. The p value is 0.01 and R² is about 0.21, so PAL explains around 21% of the variation in body fat which is a moderate amount. For women, the slope is not significant. The p value is 0.25 and R² is near zero, which means PAL has no detectable linear relationship with body fat in this sample.

The main takeaway is that higher PAL - to a moderate degree - helps predict lower body fat for men. For women, PAL did not meaningfully predict body fat.

Body Fat vs Ultra-Processed Food

This plot compares body fat percentage with the share of ultra-processed foods (UPF) in the diet. Unlike the activity plots, the points line up more clearly here. For both men and women, the relationship between UPF intake and body fat is statistically significant. The p values are 0.01 for men and 0.005 for women. The slopes are positive and similar in size, which means higher UPF intake predicts higher body fat across sexes.

For men, PRE is about 0.31. For women, it is about 0.44. These are much higher than the correlation you saw between PAL and obesity. They are still moderate, but indicate that amount of UPF consumption explains a meaningful share of the variation in body fat, especially for women. Of course, there is still variation: some groups eat relatively little UPF and have a higher body fat percentage, whereas some high-UPF groups sit below the trend line.

One additional pattern starts to emerge: populations with higher UPF intake tend to have higher HDI (Human Development Index) scores — a composite measure of a country’s income, education, and life expectancy. The reason for this is that as countries industrialize, they usually shift toward more processed and packaged foods. You can examine this by looking at the U.S. and South Africa in the plot above — both lie in the higher-UPF, higher-body-fat clusters.

It’s also worth noting that UPF data were available for only 25 of the 34 populations, so the coverage isn't complete. Even with that limitation, the relationship between UPFs and obesity appears more consistent than the that between obesity and physical activity. This doesn’t prove that UPF causes obesity, but suggests that dietary composition may align more closely with differences in body fat across populations than physical activity does.

Limitations

Dietary data cover only 25 of the 34 populations, so any dietary pattern is based on a partial sample. The analysis relies on population means, which prevents us from seeing how much variation exists within each group or how precise the averages are. The data are cross sectional and correlational, meaning they describe differences between populations at one point in time and cannot establish causation or track changes over time. Energy expenditure measurements come from different studies that used slightly different methods, adding measurement noise. These limits narrow what we can infer, but the broad patterns still point toward the need for better dietary data, long term studies, and policy approaches that address UPFs directly.

These population patterns suggest that, contrary to the belief of the majority of Americans, diet may play a far more important role in obesity than physical activity. Below we examine the biological mechanisms for this.

Examining the Genetic and Willpower Myths

1/4

The Genetic Myth

In the survey above, we have seen that many Americans believe that genetics are a key factor in the obesity epidemic. However, looking at the timespan of this epidemic (namely global obesity tripling over the last five decades) makes one point unmistakably clear: this epidemic cannot be explained by genetics. The reason for this is that genetic frequencies (the makeup of our DNA) change over thousands or even millions of years, not within a single human lifetime, meaning that our DNA today is not different in any significant way than it was 50 years ago. While there is a genetic component to individual obesity, focusing on this might seem entirely misguided given that the cause of the obesity pandemic clearly lies elsewhere.

This perspective is flawed, however, since understanding the mechanism that makes some individuals are more susceptible to obesity will help us understand mechanisms underlying the meteoric rise in the general population. Historically, scientists viewed obesity as two distinct conditions: 1) severe, early-onset obesity which is caused by detrimental alterations in single genes; and 2) 'common' obesity which results from small alterations in many genes. More recently scientists came to understand that both point towards the same underlying biological mechanism: the leptin–melanocortin pathway, a hormonal-neuronal circuit in the hypothalamus that tells your brain how much energy your body has, whether to feel hungry or full, and whether to burn calories or conserve them (i.e. it regulates your metabolism). [12] [22]

2/4

The leptin–melanocortin pathway

The preceding image visualizes this neuronal circuitry. It illustrates how hormonal signals such as leptin (released from fat tissue) and insulin (released from the pancreas) enter the brain and bind to specific neurons in the hypothalamus: POMC neurons and AgRP neurons. In POMC cells, leptin and insulin stimulate the production of α-MSH, a peptide that activates MC4R receptors on downstream neurons to promote satiety and increase energy expenditure. In AgRP neurons, by contrast, the same hormonal signals suppress firing and reduce the release of AgRP and NPY, two potent drivers of hunger and metabolic conservation. When this cascade works as intended, the brain accurately gauges the body's energy state and maintains a balanced regulation of appetite, fullness, and energy expenditure. [23]

But when perturbed – whether by a rare, high-impact mutation or the cumulative effect of many small genetic variants – this energy-sensing system becomes distorted, leading, for instance, to less responsive POMC and AgRP neurons. As a result, the hypothalamus begins to misinterpret normal or even elevated leptin levels as if they were low, effectively reading the body as under-fed when it is not. Affected individuals then receive skewed biological signals that increase hunger, blunt satiety, and reduce metabolic rate. In other words, they suffer from a biological imbalance towards higher weight. [24]

3/4

UPFs disrupt the same pathway

Importantly, the same leptin–melanocortin pathway is disrupted by diets dominated by ultra-processed foods.

UPFs are energy-dense, rapidly absorbable, and engineered to be hyper-palatable. When consumed regularly, they drive repeated spikes in glucose, insulin, inflammatory signals, and leptin. Over time, this barrage leads the hypothalamus to become progressively less responsive to leptin and insulin. [25] In this state of leptin and insulin resistance, the brain perceives the body as having less stored energy than it actually does. The result is the same as in genetically linked obesity: hunger increases, satiety decreases, and metabolic rate slows.

4/4

Why UPFs affect both sides of the energy equation

Critically, eating UPFs does not only lead to impaired satiety regulation and thus likely to a higher caloric intake, but also disrupt the other side of the energy balance equation. Chronic consumption lowers your metabolic rate by:

  • Shifting your brain towards energy conversion mode (as the hypothalamus assumes a need to conserve energy because of its resistance to leptin)
  • Causing chronic, low-grade inflammation in the hypothalamus, which suppresses basal metabolic rate. [13]
  • Requiring minimal energy to digest (e.g. 100g of almonds need ~30kcal to digest, whereas 100g of UPFs need ~5kcal). [26]
  • Lowering non-exercise caloric burn as the hypothalamus controls spontaneous movement (such as fidgeting), which you're less likely to engage in when the leptin–melanocortin pathway is impaired. [27]

The key takeaway here is that the same satiety-regulating neuronal pathway that rare gene mutations disrupt in a few individuals is disrupted by UPFs in entire populations.

Biological Impairment vs Wilpower

Above we have seen that ultra-processed foods disrupt the leptin-melanocortin pathway, and in doing so disable the body's ability to accurately regulate hunger, while also lowering your metabolic rate. What this means is that when regularly consuming UPFs overeating isn't primarily about willpower—it is the natural course of action as you simply stop receiving signals that you are full.

On the flipside, it is very unlikely to become obese when only eating non-processed foods because your body is able to accurately regulate hunger — an ability it has perfected over hundreds of thousands of years of evolution. After reaching your caloric needs, you simply don't want o eat more fruits, vegetables, chicken, or bulgur.

While it's true that fast food chains are the epitome of ultra-processing, many common foods are also substantially processed — including pasta, cheese, almost all types of bread (certainly in the US), sausage, and many protein bars. Not all processed food is necessarily 'bad' for you and vice versa, there are unprocessed foods you shouldn't have too much of. However, when it comes to satiety regulation, most (ultra-)processed foods impair this mechanism.

The satiety regulating mechanism is also why optimal diets for weight loss are both rich in unprocessed fruits and vegetables (meaning high in fiber) as well as high in (unprocessed) protein: fiber and protein are extremely good at regulating hunger. [28] [29]

GLP-1s target the same pathway

U.S. Obesity Rates (2008-2025)

1/2

Obesity Showing Signs of Decline in U.S.

Since 2022, U.S. obesity rates have been declining! Gallup's 2025 National Health and Well-Being Index reports a drop from 39.9 percent in 2022 to 37.0 percent in 2025, an estimated 7.6 million fewer adults with obesity. [18]

The decline aligns with the rapid uptake of GLP-1 drugs, which help people feel full sooner and eat less. National spending on these medications increased from $13.7 billion in 2018 to $71.7 billion in 2023. [17] Survey data indicate that roughly 40% of prescriptions are for weight management, and adult use has risen from 5.8% in early 2024 to 12.4% in 2025. [19] [18]

The steepest reductions in obesity are occurring in the very age groups with the highest GLP-1 usage (ages 40–64), suggesting these medications may be contributing to the first sustained population-level decline in U.S. obesity rates. [18]

2/2

How GLP-1s Target the Same Pathway

Importantly, the leptin–melanocortin pathway we explored above enables us to understand why GLP-1s are so effective. Although GLP-1 receptor agonists (such as semaglutide, the active drug molecule found in Ozempic and Wegovy; as well as tirzepatide, found in Rybelsus and Zepbound) do not directly repair the leptin–melanocortin pathway, they effectively bypass its dysfunction by activating downstream circuits that compensate for its impaired signaling.

GLP-1 receptors are expressed on several of the same hypothalamic neurons involved in the leptin–melanocortin system, including POMC neurons—the neurons that normally promote satiety when leptin binds to them. [21] [15] In individuals with genetic susceptibility or leptin resistance, these neurons receive a weakened signal from leptin. GLP-1 agonists step in by stimulating these satiety-promoting neurons through a different hormonal channel, restoring the fullness signal the brain would otherwise be missing.

In addition, GLP-1 agonists reduce activity in AgRP/NPY "hunger neurons," the counterparts that normally elevate appetite when the brain thinks energy stores are low. [15] By dampening these neurons and simultaneously enhancing POMC activity, GLP-1 drugs re-establish a functional imbalance in favor of satiety — essentially overriding the biased signals coming from a disrupted leptin–melanocortin system.

This dual action explains why GLP-1 medications so effectively reduce appetite, increase fullness, and shift energy expenditure in a direction that supports weight loss, even in individuals whose central regulatory circuits are environmentally (or in rare cases genetically) impaired.

UPFs disrupt and GLP-1s restore

The TL;DR is that GLP1s bypass the dysfunction of the leptin–melanocortin pathway, which is caused by regular ultra-processed food consumption and affects - to different degrees - the majority of the population in most industrialized conutries. [14] [15] By stimulating satiety-promoting neurons and dampening hunger-promoting neurons, GLP1s essentially override the dysfunctional signals that arise from leptin resistance and in doing so, help change eating behavior. [20]

This is likely why we're seeing the first slight population level obesity decline in U.S history. It's not about willpower or exercise. It's about correcting a biological dysfunction caused by consumption of ultra processed foods that drives overconsumption.

GLP-1s Are Not a Silver Bullet

While this decline in obesity rates is awesome, it does not address the underlying driver of the epidemic. GLP-1s can counteract a dysfunctional leptin–melanocortin pathway and restore satiety signals, but relying solely on medication to manage weight ignores that obesity is only one manifestation of poor dietary quality.

The goal should not simply be to eat less, it should be to eat better. UPF don't just drive weight gain; they are independently associated with cardiovascular disease, Type 2 diabetes, metabolic dysfunction, chronic inflammation, and many cancers. [16] A person on GLP-1 medication who continues consuming a diet dominated by UPFs may lose weight (which is awesome, bot for their pysical and mental health), but they remain exposed to the broader health consequences that obesity, and body fat, metrics alone don't capture.

This emphasis the importance of policy action when it comes to our food system. Specifically, the pervasive availability and aggressive marketing of ultra-processed foods that makes unhealthy eating the default choice.

Conclusion

Across this report, we challenged the widespread assumption that obesity is primarily caused by a lack of physical activity. Instead, we saw that ultra processed food consumption more accurately predict differences in body fat, because it disrupts the brain circuts that regulate satiefty. Consequently, the recent U.S. decline in obesity rates (the first sustained one in its history) can likely be attributed to the spread of GLP-1 drugs that target the downstream brain circuits UPFs disrupt.

Public discussions often blame inactivity or willpower. Some of that framing has been shaped by industry funded messaging that emphasized exercise over diet. [3] Because of the biological changes in satieety regulation from consuming UPFs, strategies focused mainly on individual behavior will have limited effect. Policies that target food environments, marketing, and UPF availability offer a stronger lever.
