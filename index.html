The Obesity Myth:

Why Our Assumptions About Obesity Deserve a Closer Look

Obesity has risen alongside economic development, but the question of what drives that rise is still contested. Scroll to explore why what Americans believe about obesity might not match the data.

A rising global pattern

Obesity has increased almost everywhere over the past few decades. Estimates from the World Health Organization indicate that global adult obesity has nearly tripled since 1975, reaching about 1 in 8 adults worldwide by 2022. Source: WHO fact sheet.

The map above shows the latest available adult obesity rates by country. Darker regions indicate a larger share of adults living with obesity, while lighter regions indicate lower levels. The data come from standardized NCD surveys, which makes it possible to compare countries directly. Source: NCD-RisC.

A few patterns to notice: high rates appear in places like the United States, the Gulf states, and several Pacific Island nations, while much of East and Southeast Asia remains on the lower end. Many countries in Africa and South Asia are still relatively low but rising quickly. Source: NCD-RisC.

Rather than a single global trend, the map suggests very different national trajectories. That raises a question we’ll return to: are these differences driven more by activity, diet, economics, or something else? How obesity has changed over time

The map above lets you click any country to explore how its obesity rates have changed across adults, women, men, and—when available—children. This time-series view uses standardized NCD-RisC estimates that track obesity prevalence from the late 1970s to today. Source: NCD-RisC.

What you’ll notice is that almost every country shows an upward trend, but the timing, slope and Y axis scale are very different. Some countries began rising early, others much later, and some increased more sharply than others. These differences are the starting point for understanding what might be driving obesity patterns around the world.

Try clicking countries with contrasting stories—like the United States, Japan, or the Gulf states. Their trajectories raise important questions about why some places surged quickly while others remained low for decades.

In the next section, we guide you through three of the most interesting examples to show how these differences set up the deeper question: What’s actually driving these trends?

A steep early rise: United States

When you look at the United States on the chart, the obesity curve starts climbing early and never really flattens. National survey data show that adult obesity has roughly tripled over the last several decades, and recent CDC estimates put adult obesity at around 40% of the population. Sources: CDC data brief (2024) , USAFacts summary .

In this view, both adults and children in the U.S. show an early, linear rise compared with many other countries. The levels are not only high today; they have been high for a long time. That makes the U.S. an example of a country where obesity became common relatively early in the global timeline.

Keep this shape in mind as a reference point when you click through to the next countries: is their curve just as steep, delayed, or surprisingly flat?

A slow, low curve: Japan

Japan’s trend looks very different. Even as a high-income country with long-standing urbanization, its adult obesity levels remain much lower than in most OECD peers. Recent analyses find that Japan has one of the lowest rates of overweight and obesity among OECD countries, and average BMI has changed only gradually over several decades. Sources: Our World in Data , Ikeda et al., 2022 (Japan BMI trends) .

On the chart, that shows up as a flatter, lower line for adults and children: obesity rises, but much more slowly and from a very low starting point. Compared with the U.S., Japan offers a contrast where economic development did not produce the same jump in obesity levels.

This contrast raises a key question for later: if income or development alone doesn’t explain the gap between the U.S. and Japan, what else might be different in their food environments or daily lives?

A rapid surge: Gulf states

In many Gulf countries, such as Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, and Bahrain, the pattern is a later but very rapid rise. Recent estimates place adult obesity levels in the high 30s to low 40s percent range, putting several of these countries among the highest in the world. Sources: World Obesity Federation rankings , Al-Nohair, 2014 (Gulf obesity overview) .

On the chart, that shows up as a sharp upswing starting later in time: obesity was relatively uncommon several decades ago, then increased quickly alongside rapid changes in income, urbanization, and food environments. In some Gulf states, women’s obesity rates in particular reach some of the highest levels globally.

Taken together, these three curves—U.S., Japan, and the Gulf—illustrate that obesity can become common early, stay low for a long time, or surge rapidly once conditions change. What is causing this rise?

At its core, obesity occurs when energy intake is consistently higher than energy expenditure. The harder question is which side of this balance has changed in modern populations. Public health organizations typically point to two possibilities: people may be burning less energy through daily activity, or they may be consuming more energy as diets shift [1]. To see how this aligns with public perception, consider what Americans say is the main reason many people are very overweight.

What do most Americans say is a very important reason many Americans are very overweight?

2/2

Lower activity in industrialized societies?

A 2006 Pew survey captured Americans’ beliefs about what was causing obesity. Most respondents pointed to personal behavior as the main driver, and they overwhelmingly pointed to one explanation in particular: not getting enough exercise. In fact, they rated lack of activity as the most important reason people are overweight, ahead of diet, marketing, or genetics [2]. While the study was conducted almost a decade ago, it highlights what was at least at that time a common hypothesis shared with health organizations, that industrialized countries are less physically active than traditional farming or foraging groups, and that activity levels may have changed as societies modernized [1].

Examining the Activity Hypothesis

Given how common this assumption is, the next step is to look at it with data. Given how common this assumption is, the next step is to look at it with actual measurements rather than beliefs. A recent cross cultural study published in PNAS did this by using the doubly labeled water method, the leading approach for measuring total daily energy expenditure in real-world settings. The researchers drew from the International Atomic Energy Agency’s global database of doubly labeled water measurements and expanded it by adding three additional pastoralist groups: the Aymara in Bolivia, the Tuvan in Siberia, and the Daasanach in northern Kenya.

The initial dataset contained more than 10,000 adults. The team then filtered out anyone with missing sex, reported health conditions, athletic status, implausibly high energy expenditure, or ages outside 18 to 60. This produced a final analytic sample of 4,213 adults across 34 population groups. These groups are specific community samples, not nationally representative estimates. The goal was to compare energy expenditure across different subsistence ecologies and economic contexts, not to characterize whole countries.

The populations span hunter gatherers, pastoralists, horticulturalists, small scale farmers, and industrialized groups. Because nonindustrialized groups do not have formal Human Development Index scores, the researchers placed them on the HDI spectrum based on their level of market integration. Industrialized populations were categorized using standard UN HDI thresholds for low, medium, and high development. HDI values were then converted into rank order, so the analysis uses each population’s relative position along the development spectrum.

Basal energy expenditure came from indirect calorimetry when available or from size-based predictive equations when not. Activity energy expenditure and physical activity level were derived from the total and basal expenditure values.

Because access to the individual level dataset requires a formal research application, this analysis uses only the publicly available population means. Working with means makes it possible to compare broad patterns across populations, but it introduces important limitations. The underlying distributions for each group are not available, so measures such as standard deviations, confidence intervals, or within-group variability cannot be calculated. As a result, the analysis can identify population-level trends across economic and lifestyle contexts, but it cannot capture how much variation exists within each group or how precise the reported averages are.

Data collection and Filtering

The map shows where the 34 sampled communities are located. A few patterns are worth keeping in mind as you interpret the results. High-HDI groups are heavily represented, especially in Europe, the US, and East Asia. These samples are larger and give more stable averages. In contrast, the subsistence groups (hunter gatherers, horticulturalists, and agropastoralist) appear in only a handful of regions and often with smaller sample sizes. They are key for comparing different lifestyles, but each point represents one community, not an entire country. This uneven distribution means the analysis can compare broad differences across economic and lifestyle contexts, but high-HDI groups carry more statistical weight and subsistence groups provide only a few anchor points.

Body fat vs Physical Activity Level (PAL)

This plot compares body fat percentage with Physical Activity Level (PAL), which is total energy expenditure divided by basal energy expenditure. PAL gives a size-adjusted measure of daily activity.

Across populations, the relationship here is weak. Some groups with higher PAL have lower body fat, but others do not, and the points are widely scattered for both men and women. High-HDI populations show especially broad variation: some are fairly active with moderate body fat, while others have similar PAL values but higher body fat.

What we can take from this is limited but useful. If physical activity were a dominant driver of population-level differences in body fat, we would expect a clearer downward trend. Instead, the variability suggests that activity alone does not explain why some populations have much higher body fat than others. This doesn’t rule out an effect of activity, it just means the pattern is not strong or consistent at this scale.

Conclusion

Taken together, the population-level data present a clearer picture of how obesity varies across different economic and lifestyle contexts. Once body size is accounted for, physical activity levels show only modest variation between populations. That makes it unlikely that changes in daily movement alone explain the large differences in obesity rates across societies. By contrast, in the subset of populations for which dietary data exist, there is a more consistent link: higher intake of ultra-processed foods (UPFs) appears associated with higher body-fat percentage. This does not prove causation, but it suggests dietary composition may matter more than activity in shaping obesity differences at population scale.

Why this matters

Public understanding of obesity tends to emphasize activity (move more, sit less) and personal responsibility. Survey data show many Americans attribute weight problems to lack of exercise or willpower. But this framing may reflect not only intuition, it may also reflect the influence of industry: some historical analyses suggest that certain food and beverage companies funded studies and public campaigns that highlighted physical inactivity as the root cause of obesity [3].

If dietary factors, especially the prevalence of UPFs, have a stronger link to obesity across populations than activity, then strategies focused mainly on individual behavior or exercise may miss a larger lever. Instead of placing the burden solely on individuals to "move more," public health efforts might be more effective if they address structural and environmental drivers: availability, marketing, formulation, and consumption of UPFs.

That's not to say individuals don't matter. Diet and activity both influence health. But orienting policy and resources around the stronger population-level signal could lead to larger-scale impact.

Limitations

Several limitations of the dataset and analysis should be noted.

  • The dietary data cover only a portion of the sample (25 of the 34 populations).
  • The analysis relies on population means, not individual-level data, so within-population variation and uncertainty (such as confidence intervals or standard deviations) cannot be assessed.
  • The data are cross-sectional, reflecting differences between populations at one point in time. This prevents us from inferring how changes over time influence body fat.
  • The associations observed are correlational, and unmeasured factors (such as genetics or other lifestyle variables) may contribute to the patterns.
  • Energy-expenditure measurements come from different studies and methods, which may introduce measurement heterogeneity.

While these limitations exist, the patterns still point toward a need for better dietary data, long-term studies, and policy approaches that address food environments directly. Earlier industry messaging often emphasized activity, but moving forward it will be important for governments and communities to take a stronger role in challenging that narrative. Framing obesity as a structural issue rather than an individual failure can open the door to more funding, better regulation, and broader efforts to reduce UPF exposure and support healthier diets.

The genetic paradox

The fact that obesity has quadrupled over the last five decades makes one point unmistakably clear: the epidemic cannot be explained by genetics alone. Genetic frequencies (the makeup of our DNA) change over thousands or even millions of years, not within a single human lifetime. While there is a genetic component to individual obesity, focusing on this might seem entirely misguided given that the cause of the obesity pandemic clearly lies elsewhere.

This perspective is flawed, however, since understanding why individuals are more susceptible to obesity might help us understand how to decrease susceptibility of everyone. Historically, scientists viewed obesity as two distinct conditions: 1) severe, early-onset obesity which is caused by detrimental alterations in single genes and 2) 'common' obesity which results from small alterations in many genes. What scientists came to understand though, is that both point towards the same underlying biological mechanism: the leptin–melanocortin pathway, a hormonal-neuronal circuit in the hypothalamus that tells your brain how much energy the body has, whether to feel hungry or full, and whether to burn calories or conserve them.

2/4

The leptin–melanocortin pathway

The preceding image visualizes this neuronal circuitry. It illustrates how hormonal signals such as leptin (released from fat tissue) and insulin (released from the pancreas) bind to specific neurons in the hypothalamus. These signals are meant to initiate a coordinated cascade: activating POMC neurons that promote satiety, while simultaneously inhibiting AgRP neurons that drive hunger. When this cascade works as intended, the brain accurately gauges the body's energy state and maintains a balanced regulation of appetite, fullness, and energy expenditure.

But when perturbed – whether by a rare, high-impact mutation or the cumulative effect of many small genetic variants – this energy-sensing system becomes distorted. The brain begins to misread the body as under-fed, even when it is not. As a result, individuals receive skewed biological signals that increase hunger, blunt satiety, and reduce metabolic rate.

UPFs disrupt the same pathway

Importantly, the same leptin–melanocortin pathway is disrupted by diets dominated by ultra-processed foods. UPFs act as a physiological stressor on the pathway, pushing it into a state of dysfunction that mimics, in a weaker form, the signaling failures seen in genetic obesity.

UPFs are energy-dense, rapidly absorbable, and engineered to be hyper-palatable. When consumed regularly, they drive repeated spikes in glucose, insulin, inflammatory signals, and leptin. Over time, this barrage leads the hypothalamus to become progressively less responsive to leptin. In this state of leptin resistance, the brain perceives the body as having less stored energy than it actually does. The result is a maladaptive shift in the same brain circuits affected by genetic variants: hunger increases, satiety decreases, and metabolic rate slows.

3/4

Why UPFs affect both sides of the energy equation

Critically, eating UPFs does not only lead to impaired satiety regulation and thus likely to a higher caloric intake, but also disrupt the other side of the energy balance equation. Chronic consumption lowers your metabolic rate by:

  • Shifting your brain towards energy conversion mode (as the hypothalamus assumes a need to conserve energy because of its resistance to leptin)
  • Causing chronic, low-grade inflammation in the hypothalamus, which suppresses basal metabolic rate
  • Requiring minimal energy to digest (e.g. 100g of almonds need ~30kcal to digest, whereas 100g of UPFs need ~5kcal)
  • Lowering non-exercise caloric burn as the hypothalamus controls spontaneous movement (such as fidgeting), which you're less likely to engage in when the leptin–melanocortin pathway is impaired

The key takeaway here is that the same satiety-regulating neuronal pathway that rare gene mutations disrupt in a few individuals is disrupted by UPFs in entire populations.

4/4

GLP-1s target the same pathway

This also explains why GLP-1s are so effective. Although GLP-1 receptor agonists (such as semaglutide and tirzepatide) do not directly repair the leptin–melanocortin pathway, they effectively bypass its dysfunction by activating downstream circuits that compensate for its impaired signaling.

GLP-1 receptors are expressed on several of the same hypothalamic neurons involved in the leptin–melanocortin system, including POMC neurons—the very neurons that normally promote satiety when leptin binds to them. In individuals with genetic susceptibility or leptin resistance, these neurons receive a weakened or distorted signal from leptin. GLP-1 agonists step in by stimulating these satiety-promoting neurons through a different hormonal channel, restoring the fullness signal that the brain would otherwise be missing.

In addition, GLP-1 agonists reduce activity in AgRP/NPY "hunger neurons," the counterparts that normally elevate appetite when the brain thinks energy stores are low. By dampening these neurons and simultaneously enhancing POMC activity, GLP-1 drugs re-establish a functional imbalance in favor of satiety—essentially overriding the biased energy-deficit signals coming from a disrupted leptin–melanocortin system.

This dual action explains why GLP-1 medications so effectively reduce appetite, increase fullness, and shift energy expenditure in a direction that supports weight loss, even in individuals whose central regulatory circuits are environmentally (or in rare cases genetically) impaired.
